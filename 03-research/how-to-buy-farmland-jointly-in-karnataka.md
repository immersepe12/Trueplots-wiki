## The short answer

Yes, it is legal to jointly buy farmland in Karnataka with 2–4 other buyers and end up with individual titles for each buyer. The process requires:

1. A joint registered sale deed at purchase
2. A partition deed post-purchase
3. Phodi (physical measurement) and mutation in revenue records
4. Creation of new survey sub-numbers (hissa) for each buyer
5. Individual khatas/pattas issued per new hissa

**Realistic timeline:** 3–9 months post-purchase. **Typical cost:** ₹40K–80K in legal + surveyor + revenue office fees per partition. **Biggest risk:** doing this informally without proper legal structuring, which creates co-ownership deadlock, unclear exit rights, and disputes that can last for decades.

## Why joint-buying is the most under-used option in South Indian farmland

The economics are compelling but the coordination is hard:

- A 7-acre premium plot in Kanakapura might be priced at ₹1.5 crore — out of reach for a single buyer with a ₹40L budget
- But four buyers at ₹40L each could happily split it into four 1.5-acre parcels
- The 7-acre plot would sell; four buyers would own premium land they otherwise couldn't afford

The catch is the legal structuring. Most informal joint-purchase attempts in India fail because buyers underestimate:

- **Finding trustworthy co-buyers** — buying with strangers carries obvious risk
- **Structuring the co-ownership** — without a partition deed, co-owners are stuck in "tenants in common" status under Section 44 of the Transfer of Property Act, which creates deadlock on any future decision
- **Dividing the land physically** — who gets which 1.5 acres? With what road access?
- **Creating separate khatas** — without individual khatas, you can't build independently, sell independently, or pay taxes cleanly
- **Handling exit scenarios** — what happens when one buyer wants to sell in year 3?

## Warning: not every "joint purchase" is legal

Several Indian platforms have offered farmland "joint ownership" as an investment product — Growpital being the most prominent. In April 2024, SEBI shut down Growpital as an **unregistered Collective Investment Scheme**, freezing funds of 5,200+ investor-partners. ([SEBI order](https://www.sebi.gov.in/legal/orders))

The red flags that classify a joint purchase as an unregistered CIS:
- Pooled money held by a platform or SPV (not by you)
- Promised or implied returns ("11–14% tax-free")
- Platform manages the land for shared profit
- You receive units or shares, not a registered sale deed

**A legal joint purchase is none of these.** Each buyer individually signs the sale deed. Money flows buyer → seller via escrow, not via platform. Each buyer receives a registered title. There are no pooled returns. This is property law, not securities law.

## The seven-step legal process in Karnataka

### Step 1: Due diligence on the plot

Before any buyer commits, verify:
- **Ownership** via Bhoomi / RTC lookup
- **Encumbrance** via EC portal and CERSAI
- **Mutation history** and chain-of-title from mother deed
- **PTCL status** — the plot was not originally SC/ST-granted land transferred without permission. Critical: PTCL claims can survive 30+ years.
- **79A/79B applicability** — as of April 2026, sections 79A and 79B of the Karnataka Land Reforms Act are **repealed**, so non-agriculturists can buy. Monitor legislative changes.
- **Land classification** — agricultural vs converted; construction allowed or requires conversion
- **Tank bed, forest land, greenbelt, CDP zoning** — all disqualifiers
- **Boundary accuracy** via KGIS cross-check
- **Road access** (must have revenue or panchayat road, not claimed-only)
- **Minimum subdivision extent** — Karnataka Fragmentation Act sets minimum holdings per taluk; subdivision below threshold requires Tahsildar exemption

### Step 2: Buyer agreement before purchase

Before signing the sale deed, all co-buyers sign a written pre-purchase agreement covering:

- Individual share of the plot (by value and by physical location)
- Payment schedule and escrow mechanics
- What happens if any buyer pulls out before registration
- Subdivision plan (how the plot will be divided post-purchase)
- Who pays legal, surveyor, and revenue office fees
- Dispute resolution mechanism
- Exit/resale rights (can one buyer sell their parcel in year 3 without the others' consent?)

This is the single most important document in the entire process. Cheap legal drafting here creates lawsuits later.

### Step 3: Joint registered sale deed

All co-buyers sign one sale deed with the seller. Each buyer pays their share of the purchase price and stamp duty + registration (typically ~6.6% in Karnataka). The plot is legally owned by all co-buyers as "tenants in common" under Section 44 of TPA.

### Step 4: Partition deed

The co-buyers execute a partition deed dividing the plot into physically demarcated parcels, with:
- Each buyer's parcel boundaries defined
- Internal road access / easement layout
- Water source allocation
- Any shared utilities
- Partition deed is registered; stamp duty typically nominal (1% of property value in Karnataka, per 2025 schedule)

### Step 5: Phodi (physical measurement) and 11E sketch

A licensed surveyor conducts phodi — physical measurement of each parcel — and prepares the **11E sketch** showing:
- Total extent
- Boundaries of each sub-parcel
- Kharab (waste) areas
- Net cultivable area

The 11E sketch is submitted to the Assistant Director of Land Records.

### Step 6: Mutation and new hissa numbers

The Assistant Director of Land Records reviews the 11E sketch and partition deed, and issues:
- New survey sub-numbers (hissa) for each parcel
- Updated RTC (Pahani) for each new hissa
- Individual khata / patta for each buyer

**Timeline:** Officially 15–30 days after 11E submission; realistically 3–6 months in clean cases, 6–12+ months with backlog or objections.

### Step 7: Registration of individual khatas

Each buyer registers their new khata with the local Gram Panchayat or municipal authority (depending on plot location). Property tax assessments are issued per new hissa.

End state: 4 buyers, 4 legally independent parcels, 4 individual khatas, shared legal road access via registered easement, zero co-ownership entanglement.

## The most common mistakes

1. **Skipping the pre-purchase agreement.** Co-buyers shake hands, sign the sale deed, and then discover they disagree on subdivision boundaries, exit rules, or maintenance contributions. This is the #1 source of joint-purchase lawsuits.
2. **Not verifying PTCL status.** A 1978-grant-land-to-SC/ST-family can surface as a claim 40 years later. Recent 2024 amendments have slightly tightened the statute of limitations, but PTCL diligence remains non-negotiable.
3. **Assuming conversion is optional.** If the plot is agricultural and any buyer wants to build a farmhouse larger than what farmhouse-construction rules allow, conversion is required. This is a separate 3–6 month process after purchase.
4. **Ignoring minimum extent rules.** Karnataka Fragmentation Act sets minimum holdings (often 1 acre irrigated / 2 acres dry). If your subdivision creates parcels below the threshold, Tahsildar exemption is required — not guaranteed.
5. **Taking title through an SPV or LLP.** This turns your property purchase into a potential Collective Investment Scheme under SEBI jurisdiction. Keep it simple: individual registered sale deeds per buyer.
