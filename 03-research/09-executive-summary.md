## The five decisions this analysis should drive

### 1. Is fractional farmland with individual titles in South India genuine whitespace?

**Yes at the stranger-pool tier, no at the developer-pool tier.**

- **At the stranger-pool tier** (strangers form a group, co-purchase, post-sale partition to individual titles): no Indian player operating at scale. Confirmed. This is TruePlots' defensible whitespace. [[01-research-notes.md](01-research-notes.md#part-a)]
- **At the developer-pool tier** (developer pre-subdivides and sells slices): **Saptashwa Jagati Farms is already doing this in Kanakapura at ₹45.74L/slice, actively marketed on 99acres April 2026.** This contradicts the brief's framing and should trigger a Section 10 revision. [[02-competitor-profiles/saptashwa-jagati-farms.yaml](02-competitor-profiles/saptashwa-jagati-farms.yaml)]
- **Internationally:** only Farmfolio (Colombia) operates individual-title fractional farmland. Every US/AU/NZ/EU platform uses SPV/LP structures. TruePlots' individual-title model is structurally rare and property-law (not securities-law) — which is both a moat and a constraint. [[01-research-notes.md](01-research-notes.md#part-b)]

### 2. What's the realistic defensibility window before a well-funded competitor copies?

**12–18 months**, not 24+. Top threats in order: [[04-whitespace-analysis.md](04-whitespace-analysis.md)]

1. **Hasiru Farms** pivoting to add marketplace breadth (they have buyer trust, supply relationships, and already publish diligence content)
2. **Farmland Bazaar** adding a verification layer (90-day play with one lawyer partnership)
3. **New well-funded entrant** (Growpital precedent slows SPV-based copies)

The defensibility flip by month 12 must be from "we're the only one" to "we're operationally most mature" — needs 30+ clean fractional deals executed in year 1.

### 3. Which positioning should TruePlots commit to?

**Option B — Access-First.** Positioning: *"Own verified farmland — by yourself, or with others."* [[06-positioning-options.md](06-positioning-options.md)]

- Against A (Trust-First): verification is copyable in 90 days. Leading with a copyable moat is a marketing choice, not a strategic one.
- Against C (Category-Creating): OS-for-farmland is correct long-term but operationally deadly in year 1 (three business models, three P&Ls, fragmented attention).
- For B: structurally harder to copy (legal operations + CIS-proof architecture + affinity-group acquisition), genuine category creation (no one serves the ₹25–60L segment with premium inventory), higher margin per deal (3–4× services amplification), and press-ready narrative.
- Key validation to run: prove affinity-group acquisition works on 5 pilots. If paid CAC × 4 buyers, Option B breaks.

### 4. What are the 3–5 things the founder is probably wrong about?

In severity order: [[07-blind-spots.md](07-blind-spots.md)]

1. **Saptashwa Jagati Farms is running a variant of the thesis now.** Section 10 needs revision.
2. **79A/79B repeal is foundational, not background.** A Congress restoration vote = ~70% TAM collapse. One electoral cycle away.
3. **Verification is not a moat.** Brief acknowledges but still structures MVP around it. Reverse the sequencing.
4. **Retail CAC × 4 buyers breaks fractional unit economics** unless acquisition is affinity-group-based (family, colleagues, apartment-WhatsApp groups). AcreTrader raised $60M and got acquired; hBits has 100K users for ₹500cr AUM.
5. **Subdivision timeline (3–9 months) is structurally harder than acknowledged.** First 50–100 deals will be founder/senior-team heavy, not 5–10. Budget for a senior land-ops lead from founding team.

### 5. What are realistic unit economics for the first 20 deals?

[[05-pricing-benchmarks.md](05-pricing-benchmarks.md)]

**Fractional deal (₹1.5cr 7-acre plot, 4 buyers @ ₹37.5L each):**
- Transaction commission: 1.5–2% of ₹1.5cr = ₹2.25–3L (seller-side)
- Fractional coordination fee: ₹50–60K × 4 buyers = ₹2–2.4L
- Services revenue (registration, conversion, referral margins): ₹1–2L
- **Gross revenue per fractional deal: ₹5.5–7L**
- Legal + surveyor + revenue office cost: ₹1–1.5L
- CAC (if affinity-group): ₹60K–1L per deal total
- CAC (if paid-acquisition × 4): ₹2–3L per deal → deal breakeven or loss
- **Net per fractional deal in affinity model: ₹3–4.5L**. Need ~30 deals/year to hit ₹1cr revenue.

**Whole-plot deal (₹80L, 1 buyer):**
- Transaction commission: 1.5–2% = ₹1.2–1.6L
- Services: ₹50K–1.5L
- **Gross per whole-plot deal: ₹1.7–3L**. CAC ₹60K–1L.

Fractional is ~2× economics per acre vs whole-plot IF acquisition is affinity-group. If acquisition becomes stranger-by-stranger, fractional is *worse* economics than whole-plot.

## The three actions this analysis recommends

1. **Revise brief v0.3 Section 10** using the [updated landscape map](03-landscape-map-updated.md). Add Saptashwa. Upgrade Farmland Bazaar threat. Add Growpital and PACL as regulatory precedent. Add 79A/79B monitoring as Top-3 risk, not a passing mention.

2. **Run the affinity-group acquisition validation before month 6.** Pick 5 pilot fractional deals. Track: for each of 4 buyers in each deal, how did they find TruePlots? If ≥50% came via paid acquisition rather than referral from another buyer, the fractional unit economics are broken and the product needs redesign (pre-subdivision model, B2B2C via employer networks, or abandonment of stranger-pooling).

3. **Hire a senior land-ops lead as founding team member, not month-6 hire.** Ex-Karnataka revenue department consultant or ex-managed-farmland operations head. The first 50 fractional deals are operationally heavy; founder cannot be both CEO and full-time Tahsildar liaison.
