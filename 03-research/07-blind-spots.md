# Highest-Risk Blind Spots

*Things the brief is probably getting wrong. Should feel uncomfortable to read. Ranked by severity.*

---

## 1. "No one is doing this in India" is empirically false — Saptashwa is

**Brief v0.2 section 10.1:** "No established fractional-farmland platform operating in India that performs legal subdivision into individual titles."

**Reality (April 2026, 99acres Kanakapura):** Saptashwa Jagati Farms is actively marketing a 15-acre Kanakapura estate as ~600-sqyd slices at ₹45.74 lakh each, with "Non-Profit-Sharing model ensuring complete ownership" language. Multiple listings. Real money flowing. In TruePlots' flagship geography at TruePlots' exact target ticket.

The distinction the brief relies on — "developer pre-subdivides vs strangers form pool post-sale" — is real legally and operationally but probably **invisible to the buyer**. A ₹40L buyer choosing between a Saptashwa slice and a TruePlots fractional share sees: individual title, Kanakapura farmland, ~₹40L price. Same product from their seat.

This matters because:
- The whitespace claim is the core thesis of the brief
- A competitor is already capturing the 2–3× premium TruePlots targets
- Brief's Section 10 needs material revision

**What to do:** Actually buy into (mystery-shop) Saptashwa. Understand their legal structure, margin stack, and buyer acquisition. Decide whether TruePlots' post-sale-subdivision model is genuinely differentiated or just operationally harder for the same outcome. The answer may be that **TruePlots should also pre-subdivide** on selected plots (acquire, subdivide, sell) — eating capital efficiency for operational simplicity.

## 2. 79A/79B repeal is foundational, not background

**Brief treatment:** Section 13.1 lists this as one of several structural risks: "Real estate regulators (RERA) do not currently cover farmland in most states — but this could change."

**Reality:** Sections 79A/79B of the Karnataka Land Reforms Act — which restricted non-agriculturists from buying farmland — were **REPEALED in July 2020**. That repeal is the ENTIRE REASON urban professionals can buy the plots TruePlots is selling to Segment A and B. The Siddaramaiah Congress govt has publicly announced intent to restore these sections. They haven't yet because of Legislative Council arithmetic — which can change in one electoral cycle.

If 79A/79B are restored:
- Non-farmers (most of TruePlots' buyer base) cannot legally own agricultural land in Karnataka
- Conversion-before-purchase becomes mandatory for TruePlots' target buyers → blows up the unit economics
- The fractional product specifically collapses because ₹40L buyers don't want converted plots (which cost 7–10× more)

This is not a background risk — it's the single largest TAM-collapse scenario TruePlots faces.

**What to do:** Build a monitoring process for Karnataka Legislative Council composition and draft amendments. Have an operational response ready (pivot to converted-land + layout-approved inventory). Consider whether TAM diversification toward Tamil Nadu (different regime) should be Phase 1 instead of Phase 2.

## 3. Verification is not a moat — lead with fractional instead

**Brief treatment:** Section 5.3 explicitly names verification as "not uniquely defensible." But Section 1 leads with "every plot is legally verified before a buyer sees it" as the primary promise, and the proposed MVP v1.0 (months 1–3) is pure verification marketplace.

**Reality:** Farmland Bazaar can add verification in 90 days with one lawyer partnership. Hasiru Farms already publishes "10 Costly Errors" educational content that does much of the verification work competitively. MagicBricks could commission a verification product if farmland volume justified it. **Leading with verification means competing in a 12-month window before commoditization.**

The brief itself acknowledges this but still structures MVP around it.

**What to do:** Reverse the MVP sequencing. Either (a) collapse v1.0 and v1.5 — launch with 10 verified listings AND 1 pilot fractional deal in month 3, not verification-only — or (b) accept that the first 6 months are "verified marketplace" and be honest internally that you're not building the moat yet, just buying time.

## 4. Retail CAC for stranger fractional buyers probably breaks the unit economics

**Brief treatment:** Section 6.6.4 claims 4× services revenue per fractional deal as a unit-economics advantage. Section 11.4 acknowledges early deals will be "founder-heavy" but doesn't address acquisition cost structurally.

**Reality:** If 4 buyers must each be acquired independently via paid search/ads at ~₹60K CAC each, you spend ₹2.4L CAC to earn ₹2–3L coordination fee. Thin margin at best, loss-making likely. **AcreTrader raised $60M Series B, placed $406M, and got acquired by Proterra in August 2025** because retail fractional farmland unit economics are structurally thin. hBits has 100K registered users for ₹500cr AUM = abysmal conversion. PropShare Platina SM REIT IPO was initially subscribed 9%.

The only version of the fractional model that has healthy unit economics is **affinity-group acquisition** — 1 paid-acquisition lead brings 2–3 co-buyer friends/colleagues. This is a specific assumption about Indian professional network behavior that is plausible but unproven.

**What to do:** Before building fractional productization (v1.5 → v2.0), run 5 pilot deals with explicit attribution tracking of how each of 4 buyers arrived. If >50% require independent acquisition, the fractional product as currently scoped will bleed money at scale. Pivot to affinity-group GTM (B2B2C via employers, apartment-WhatsApp groups, professional associations) or abandon strangers pooling for developer pre-subdivision.

## 5. Subdivision timeline (3–9 months) is load-bearing and understated

**Brief treatment:** Section 6.6.3 lists the legal mechanics but doesn't emphasize timeline. Section 13.2 mentions "operational scalability" as a risk but in soft terms.

**Reality:** Partition deed → phodi → mutation → new hissa numbers → individual khatas takes **3–6 months in clean cases, 6–12+ months with Tahsildar backlog or objections**. For the full duration:
- Buyers have committed ₹40L each and are in escrow or joint provisional title
- Any one buyer's pullout stalls the whole group
- Any regulatory delay (and there will be many on the first 20 deals) means ₹1.5cr+ of customer capital sitting in limbo
- The Karnataka Fragmentation Act has minimum-extent rules that require Tahsildar exemption below certain sizes — a judgment call that can be denied
- PTCL diligence can turn up late-stage claims that void the whole partition

Brief v0.2 says "first 5-10 fractional deals will be operationally heavy." Realistic estimate: **first 50–100 deals are founder/senior-team heavy** because each one teaches you about a new Tahsildar quirk, a new revenue-record discrepancy, a new subdivision objection pattern.

**What to do:** Budget for a senior operations lead (ex-land lawyer or revenue-department consultant) as founding team member, not month-6 hire. Build escrow + refund SLA into buyer contract upfront — a buyer should know exactly what happens if subdivision fails at month 8. Consider hybrid model: pre-subdivide on owned/optioned parcels for the first 20 deals (Saptashwa model), layer on stranger-pooling only after the subdivision process is templated.

## Honorable mentions (medium severity)

- **Brand naming risk.** "TruePlots" is semantically fine but phonetically collides with Growpital in the Indian fractional-farmland press cycle. If Growpital returns to news (SEBI final order, investor refunds) any "True" prefix may get guilt-by-association.
- **The "services amplification" math assumes buyers bundle services with TruePlots.** If buyers take title and then hire their own lawyer for registration and their own contractor for construction (as most sophisticated buyers do), the 4× services revenue per fractional deal is aspirational, not captured.
- **The Farmland Bazaar threat is underestimated.** Brief says "worth deeper scrutiny." Reality: 4-year head start, 50k acres listed, press footprint as "Emerging PropTech Leader." Could close the verification gap in one quarter with proper funding.
